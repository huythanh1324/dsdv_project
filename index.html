Share of land covered by forest in five continents (%)

This chart provides a visual representation of how forest area as a percentage of land area has changed over time across various countries and regions, offering insights into global and regional trends in forest conservation and land use.

Africa and America have a down trend in percentage of land that covered by forest when the Asian witness the opposite trend. Moreover, forest in Australia and Europe seem to have recovery signal.

The continuous increase in forest area in Asia since 1990 is largely driven by extensive afforestation and reforestation programs, government policies promoting forest expansion, and efforts to combat deforestation through sustainable practices and conservation initiatives. Additionally, economic growth has enabled investments in environmental restoration and management

Sea Level Rise

Rising sea levels are threatening coastal ecosystems and human settlements.

The chart shows the rise in global sea levels over time. It highlights a steady increase in sea levels since the late 19th century, with more rapid rises observed in recent decades due to temperature.

From 1940 to 2020, there is a strong correlation between rising global temperatures and increasing sea levels. As global temperatures have increased due to greenhouse gas emissions have contributed to a steady rise in sea levels. The data indicates that as the rate of temperature rise has accelerated, particularly since the late 20th century, the rate of sea level rise has also increased, highlighting the impact of climate change on oceanic and coastal systems.

Global mean sea level rise (mm) and temperature (C) is measured relative to the 1940 - 2020 average sea level.

Natural disater

Climate change is causing shifts in ecosystems, affecting biodiversity and species survival.

Global number of reported disasters by size per decade from 1940 to 2020

The chart shows the global number of reported natural disasters per decade, categorized by the size of the disaster. It indicates an increasing trend in the frequency of natural disasters over the decades.

‘Small’ events have 5 or fewer deaths, less than 1,500 people affected, or reported economic damages below US$13 million. ‘Large’ events have at least 50 deaths, more than 150,000 people affected, or reported economic damages of at least US$320 million

Rising global temperatures contribute to the increasing frequency and intensity of natural disasters. Warmer temperatures lead to more extreme weather events, such as hurricanes, floods, heatwaves, and wildfires. The enhanced evaporation rates and increased moisture in the atmosphere fuel more intense storms and precipitation events. Additionally, higher temperatures exacerbate droughts and increase the risk of forest fires, while melting ice caps and glaciers contribute to sea-level rise, which amplifies the impact of coastal flooding and storm surges.
